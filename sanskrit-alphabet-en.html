Sanskrit

संस्कृतम् saṃskṛtam

The literal meaning of संस्कृत is "sacred, purified, perfected," hence Sanskrit is called the sacred language. It is one of the world's oldest languages, belonging to the Indo-Iranian branch of the Indo-European language family. It was the primary language of ancient Indian religion, philosophy, literature and science.

Devanagari (देवनागरी devanāgarī) is the most commonly used script for writing Sanskrit, and is also used for languages such as Hindi, Marathi and Nepali.

The International Alphabet of Sanskrit Transliteration (IAST) is the process of converting Devanagari script to Latin letters following international standards for academic research and international exchange. Additionally, ISO-15919 has some differences in transliteration: r̥ r̥̄ l̥ l̥̄ ē ō aṁ

Pronunciation Basics

0.1 Points of Pronunciation

उच्चारण-स्थान uccāraṇa-sthāna

  • Guttural (soft palate) kaṇṭhaḥ
  • Palatal (hard palate) tālu
  • Cerebral (retroflex) mūrdhā
  • Dental dantāḥ
  • Labial (lips) oṣṭhau
  • Nasal nāsikā
  • Tongue root jihvāmūlam

0.2 Length of Pronunciation

काल kāla

Classical Sanskrit simplified to use two pronunciation lengths:

  • Short vowels ह्रस्व hrasva have a duration of one syllable unit eka-mātrā
  • Long vowels दीर्घ dīrgha have a duration of two syllable units dvi-mātrā

Vedic Sanskrit additionally uses prolonged vowels

Prolonged vowels प्लुत pluta are marked with 3 at the end, e.g.: अ a आ ā आ३ ā3

0.3 Accent

स्वराः svarāḥ

  • Mixed tone स्वरित svarita
  • High tone उदात्त udātta
  • Low tone अनुदात्त anudātta

0.4 Nasality

आनुनासिक्यम् ānunāsikyam

All sounds can be nasalised.

Based on these characteristics, each sound may have 18 forms, e.g.:

अ॑ a॑ आ॑ ā̍ आ॑३ अँ॑ आँ॑ आँ॑३

अ a आ ā आ३ अँ a̐ आँ आँ३

अ॒ a॒ आ॒ ā̱ आ॒३ अँ॒ आँ॒ आँ॒३

1. Sanskrit Alphabet

संस्कृत अक्षर saṃskṛta akṣara

The Sanskrit alphabet is called वर्णमाला varṇamālā, literally meaning "garland of letters." Sanskrit is essentially a phonetic language where the written form of letters directly corresponds to unique sounds (कार kāra), and the name of the letter is its pronunciation.

Traditional Sanskrit consists of 16 vowels and 33 consonants.

1.1 Vowels

स्वरः svaraḥ

Vowels are sounds produced without obstruction of airflow through the mouth during pronunciation, also called sonants.

Devanagari is written from left to right, with a horizontal line (षिरोरेखा ṣirorekhā) connecting each letter.

When written alone, vowels take their pronunciation written form, e.g.: अ आ इ ई उ ऊ ऋ ॠ ऌ ॡ ए ऐ ओ औ

Short and long sounds are similar, with some additional distinguishing features.

There are 16 vowels in Sanskrit:

5 short vowels: अ इ उ ऋ ऌ and their 5 long vowels: आ ई ऊ ॠ ॡ where अ a इ i उ u are primary vowels

4 compound vowels: ए ऐ ओ औ

2 support vowels: अं अः

1.1.1 Simple vowels

समानाक्षर samānākṣara

Vowels produced using only one point of articulation are called simple vowels or monophthongs.

अ a इ i उ u ऋ ṛ ऌ ḷ

When the sound length is doubled, they become long vowels: आ ā ई ī ऊ ū ॠ ṝ ॡ ḹ

1.1.2 Compound vowels

सन्ध्यक्षर sandhyakṣara

Vowels produced using two points of articulation are called compound vowels or diphthongs. Compound vowels are always long.

ए e ऐ ai ओ o औ au

1.1.3 Support vowels

उभयक्स्हर ubhayakshara

Two specific sounds added to normal vowels in syllables. For demonstration purposes, the basic vowel अ a is usually added before support vowels.

अं aṃ is called After-sound

अनुस्वारः anusvāraḥ

Symbol: Written as ं in Devanagari, transliterated as ṃ e.g.: क + ं = कं (ka+ṃ=kaṃ)

Indicates nasalisation of the preceding vowel, or transforms into the corresponding nasal consonant depending on the following consonant. Before sibilants of the same group, it is pronounced as the nasal of that group; as a final sound it is pronounced as m.

अः aḥ is called Release

विसर्गः visargaḥ

Symbol: Written as ः in Devanagari, transliterated as ḥ e.g.: क + ः = कः (ka+ḥ=kaḥ)

A voiceless glottal fricative breath sound, usually appearing at word endings, pronounced as an echo of the preceding vowel, e.g.: aḥ pronounced aha, ūḥ pronounced ūhu, aiḥ pronounced aihi.

1.2 Consonants

व्यञ्जनम् vyañjanam

Consonants are sounds produced with obstruction of airflow through the mouth during pronunciation, also called consonants.

Independent written form of consonants: क ख ग घ ङ च छ ज झ ञ ट ठ ड ढ ण त थ द ध न प फ ब भ म य र ल व श ष स ह

For pronunciation convenience, consonants carry the default vowel अ a e.g.: क is transliterated as ka not k

To eliminate the default vowel, the halant mark ् (विराम virāma) is used e.g.: क ka → क् k

There are 33 consonants in Sanskrit, which can be divided into 3 types by airflow: stops sparśāḥ, semivowels antaḥstha, and sibilants ūṣmāṇaḥ

Sanskrit consonants can be classified or grouped (वर्ग varga) by point of articulation (sthāna) and manner of articulation (prayatna)

By point of articulation, they can be divided into 5 categories, corresponding to different positions in the mouth and tongue:

  • Guttural कण्ठ्य kaṇṭhya or ka-varga: ka kha ga gha ṅa ha
  • Palatal तालव्य tālavya or ca-varga: ca cha ja jha ña ya śa
  • Retroflex मूर्धन्य mūrdhanya or ṭa-varga: ṭa ṭha ḍa ḍha ṇa ra ṣa
  • Dental दन्त्य dantya or ta-varga: ta tha da dha na la sa
  • Labial ओष्ठ्य oṣṭhya or pa-varga: pa pha ba bha ma va

By manner of articulation they can be divided into:

1.2.1 Plosives

स्पृष्ट spṛṣṭa

Characteristics: Complete obstruction of airflow followed by sudden release

Classification:

  • Voiceless अघोषः aghoṣāḥ
  • Voiced घोषवन्तः ghoṣavantaḥ

Each has aspirated महाप्राणाः mahāprāṇāḥ and unaspirated अल्पप्राणाः alpaprāṇāḥ distinctions

  • Unaspirated voiceless: ka ca ṭa ta pa
  • Aspirated voiceless: kha cha ṭha tha pha
  • Unaspirated voiced: ga ja ḍa da ba
  • Aspirated voiced: gha jha ḍha dha bha

1.2.2 Nasals

आनुनासिकाः anunāsikāḥ

Characteristics: Airflow resonates through the nasal cavity

ṅa ña ṇa na ma

Example: ṇka

1.2.3 Semivowels

अन्तःस्थ antaḥstha

Characteristics: Gliding sounds between vowels and consonants

ya ra la va

Example: yoga

1.2.4 Sibilants

ऊष्माणः ūṣmāṇaḥ

Characteristics: Friction produced as air passes through a narrow channel

śa ṣa sa ha

Example: śiva

1.2.5 Special Consonants

  • Retroflex lateral: ळ ḻa ळ्ह ḻha (found in Vedic Sanskrit, rarely used in Classical Sanskrit)
  • Visarga विसर्गः visargaḥ final breath sound
  • Anusvāra अनुस्वारः anusvāraḥ nasalisation

1.3 Conjunct Letters

संयुक्ताक्षर saṃyuktākṣara

Conjunct letters are groups of consecutive consonants without vowels, usually combined with vowels to form complete pronunciation units.

1.3.1 Rules of Pronunciation

Assimilation

Consonants in combination may change pronunciation to adapt to adjacent consonants, e.g.:

  • t + s → tsa sound change like ts in English "cats"
  • n + c → ñca nasal assimilation, n becomes palatal nasal ñ

Aspiration and Voicing

When aspirated and unaspirated consonants combine, aspiration may weaken.

Voiceless consonants followed by voiced consonants may become voiced, e.g.: t + da → dda

Nasalisation

When nasals combine with stops, they are often accompanied by nasal resonance, e.g. mpa

1.3.2 Rules of Writing

When consonant combinations have vertical halant lines, the vertical line disappears, e.g.: स्+त→स्त s+ta→sta / ष्+ट+ष्ट ṣ+ṭa→ṣṭa

When consonant combinations have right-curved halant letters, the right curve disappears by half, e.g.: क्+न→क्न k+na→kna

Special case: र् r in first position, written as a symbol above: र्+त→र्त r+ta→rta / र्+न→र्न r+na→rna

र ra in second position, symbol written at bottom left, but circular letters retain halant: क्+र→क्र k+ra→kra / प्+र→प्र p+ra→pra / ट्+र→ट्र ṭ+ra→ṭra

Based on position and manner of consonant combination, they can be divided into:

Homorganic consonant combinations

Consonants with the same point of articulation combine, e.g.:

  • त् + त → त्त t + ta → tta doubled consonant, prolonged stop
  • mma prolonged bilabial nasal m

Heterorganic consonant combinations

Consonants with different points of articulation combine,

  • Semivowel + consonant: न् + च → न्च n + ca → yca
  • Nasal + consonant: न् + त → न्त n + ta → nta

1.3.3 Special Conjunct Consonants

क्ष (kṣa) त्र (tra) ज्ञ (jña) श्र (śra) are high-frequency conjunct consonants unique to Sanskrit that need to be memorised separately.

  • क्(k) + ष(ṣa) → क्ष (kṣa)
  • त्(t) + र(ra) → त्र (tra)
  • ज्(j) + ञ(ña) → ज्ञ (jña)
  • श्(ś) + र(ra) → श्र (śra)

1.4 Consonants with Vowels

स्वरयुक्तव्यञ्जनानि svarayuktavyañjanāni

The combined form of consonants and vowels differs from independent writing. Written vowels are systematically attached to consonants in different positions (after, before, below, above the consonant) as symbols.

1.4.1 Vowel Symbols

मत्र matra

अ आ ा इ ि ई ी उ ु ऊ ू ऋ ृ ॠ ॄ ऌ ॢ ॡ ॣ ए े ऐ ै ओ ो औ ौ

E.g.: क ka; का kā; कि ki; की kī; कु ku; कू kū; के ke; कै kai; को ko; कौ kau

Special cases: Syllables combined with r or ṛ, e.g.: रु ru रू rū रृ rṛ हृ hṛ हॄ hṝ

1.4.2 Special Marks

Nasalisation आनुनासिक्यम् ānunāsikyam

Crescent dot (चन्द्रबिन्दु candra-bindu), marked as ँ transliterated as m̐ e.g.: क + ँ = कँ (ka+m̐=kam̐)

Half-moon (अर्धचन्द्र ardha-candra), marked as ॅ transliterated as ṁ e.g.: क + ॅ = कॅ (ka+ṁ=kaṁ)

Punctuation

  • । Vertical bar (दण्ड daṇḍa) transliterated as: । and ॥ indicates end of sentence or midpoint of verse; double vertical bar indicates end of paragraph or verse.
  • ऽ Elision mark (अवग्रह avagraha) transliterated as: ' used to separate elements in compound words, indicates vowel deletion due to liaison, long vowels are deleted, elision mark can be repeated.

Examples:

अथ योगानुशासनम्

atha yogā'nuśāsanaṃ

तदा ट्रष्टुः स्वरूपेऽवसथानम्

tadā draṣṭuḥ svarūpe'vasthānam

प्रत्यक्षानुमानागमाः प्रमाणानि

pratyakṣā'numānā''gamāḥ pramāṇāni

1.5 Syllables

अक्षराणि akṣarāṇi

The syllable is the basic unit of Sanskrit pronunciation. Syllables are divided by vowels, each syllable having only one vowel.

  1. Types of syllables
    • Open syllable: ends with a vowel, e.g.: क ka
    • Closed syllable: ends with a consonant, e.g.: म् m
  2. The core of a syllable is the vowel

    Each syllable must contain one vowel. The vowel can be an independent letter (वर्ण varṇa), or combined with a consonant.

    E.g.: अ a is an independent vowel syllable. क ka is a syllable composed of consonant क् k and vowel अ a.

  3. Consonants combine with vowels to form syllables

    क् k + अ a = क ka; क् k + इ i = कि ki; क् k + उ u = कु ku

  4. When two or more consonants combine, they form a conjunct consonant and combine with one vowel to form a syllable

    E.g.: क् k + ष ṣ + अ a = क्ष kṣa

  5. Other sounds at the end belong to the final syllable

    E.g.: सुखम् → सु खम् sukham → su kham

  6. Support vowels and vowels are in the same syllable

    E.g.: संस्कृत → सं स्कृ त saṃskṛta → saṃ skṛ ta

  7. Syllables with long vowels, support sounds, or consonant clusters are heavy syllables गुरु guru; others are light syllables लघु laghu

    Usually heavy syllables have twice the pronunciation duration of light syllables.

    Symbols: heavy - light ⏑ can be light or heavy ⏓

1.6 Sandhi (Junction)

सन्धि sandhi

Refers to phonetic change rules between words or within syllables of words. These rules make pronunciation more fluent and natural.

Sanskrit sandhi rules are very complex, covering changes in vowels, consonants and compound sounds.

1.6.1 Vowel Sandhi

When two vowels meet, they merge, replace or insert consonants according to vowel type to achieve fluent pronunciation.

Core principles:

  • Priority merging: Short vowels are often replaced by long vowels or compound vowels
  • Simplified pronunciation: Avoid vowel repetition, reduce pronunciation breaks through sound changes

Sandhi rules:

Same vowel merging

Two identical vowels meeting merge into a long vowel

  • अ + अ → आ
  • a + a → ā
  • इ + इ → ई
  • i + i → ī
  • उ + उ → ऊ
  • u + u → ū
  • ऋ + ऋ → ॠ
  • ṛ + ṛ → ṝ
  • ऌ + ऌ → ॡ
  • ḷ + ḷ → ḹ

Example sentences:

देव + आत्म → देवात्म

deva + ātma → devātma (god and soul → divine spirit)

अथ योग अनुशासनम् → अथ योगानुशासनम्

atha yoga anuśāsanaṃ → atha yogā'nuśāsanaṃ

Different vowel merging

When different vowels meet, sound changes occur

  • (अ, आ) + (इ, ई) → ए
  • (a, ā) + (i, ī) → e
  • (अ, आ) + ए → ऐ
  • (a, ā) + e → ai
  • (अ, आ) + (उ, ऊ) → ओ
  • (a, ā) + (u, ū) → o
  • (अ, आ) + ओ → औ
  • (a, ā) + o → au
  • (अ, आ) + (ऋ, ॠ) → अर्
  • (a, ā) + (ṛ, ṝ) → ar
  • (अ, आ) + अर् → आर्
  • (a, ā) + ar → ār
  • (अ, आ) + ऌ → अल्
  • (a, ā) + ḷ → al
  • (अ, आ) + अल् → आल्
  • (a, ā) + al → āl
  • इ, ई + अ, आ → य, आय् अ
  • i, ī + a, ā → ya, āy a
  • उ, ऊ + अ, आ → व
  • u, ū + a, ā → va
  • ए, ऐ + अ, आ → अय, आय् अ
  • e, ai + a, ā → aya, āy a
  • ओ, औ + अ, आ → अव, आव् अ
  • o, au + a, ā → ava, āv a
  • ए + अ → अ + इ + अ → अय् अ
  • e + a → a + i + a → ay a
  • ओ + अ → अ + उ + अ → अव् अ
  • o + a → a + u + a → av a

Example sentences:

पाद + उत्तानासन → पादोत्तानासन

pāda + uttāna → pādottānā

मरीचि + आसन → मरीच्यासन

marīci + āsana → marīcyāsana

Compound vowel splitting

Compound vowels ऐ ai and औ au may split into अ a + इ i or अ a + उ u for sandhi

  • ऐ + अ → अ + अ + इ + अ → आय् अ
  • ai + a → a + a + i + a → āy a
  • औ + अ → अ + अ + उ + अ → आव् अ
  • au + a → a + a + u + a → āv a

Long vowel priority rule

If both preceding and following vowels are long, priority is given to retaining the following vowel

1.6.2 Consonant Sandhi

When consonants meet consonants or vowels, to eliminate pronunciation breaks, assimilation, voicing, nasalisation and other sound changes occur according to coordination of point and manner of articulation.

Core principles:

  • Assimilation priority: Preceding consonant is assimilated to following consonant's point of articulation or voicing attribute.
  • Simplified pronunciation: Complex consonant clusters may merge into conjunct consonants, e.g.: क्(k) + ष(ṣa) → क्ष (kṣa)

Sandhi rules:

Homorganic consonant assimilation

If preceding and following consonants have same point of articulation, preceding consonant is assimilated to following consonant

  • त् + न = न्न
  • t + na = nna
  • तत् + नाम → तन्नाम
  • tat + nāma → tannāma
  • द् + न = न्न
  • d + na = nna
  • तद् + नाम → तन्नम
  • tad + nāma → tannāma
  • तत् + अपि → तद् अपि
  • tat + api → tad api (that also → even so) consonant sandhi t → d voiced by following vowel influence

Voicing assimilation

Preceding consonant's voicing attribute matches following consonant

tat + gacchati → tadgacchati

ud + tiṣṭhati → uttiṣṭhati

Aspiration weakening

When aspirated consonants combine with other consonants, aspiration weakens or disappears

path + ta → patta

Nasalisation

When consonants are followed by nasals, they become corresponding nasals

When nasals are followed by stop consonants, nasals transform into nasal consonants of corresponding point of articulation

sam + prayoga → samprayoga

ahaṃ + karomi → ahaṅkaromi

Special conjunct consonants

High-frequency conjunct consonants that need to be memorised separately in their fixed forms

  • क्(k) + ष(ṣa) → क्ष (kṣa)
  • त्(t) + र(ra) → त्र (tra)
  • ज्(j) + ञ(ña) → ज्ञ (jña)
  • श्(ś) + र(ra) → श्र (śra)

    • Pronunciation note for conjunct consonants: For the high-frequency conjunct consonant ज्ञ (jña), it's worth noting that in modern practical pronunciation (especially in Hindi and yoga terminology), it is often simplified to the sound "gya". For example, the word ज्ञान (jñāna, knowledge/wisdom) is often pronounced as gyāna in practice, the word विज्ञान (vijñāna, science) is often pronounced as vigyāna in practice, and the word प्रज्ञा (prajñā, wisdom) is often pronounced as pragyā in practice. Beginners understanding this will help with practical spelling and pronunciation.

1.6.3 Visarga Sandhi

When word-final ḥ is followed by another word, sound changes occur according to the following phoneme to achieve fluent pronunciation.

Sandhi rules:

Sound changes before vowels

  • ḥ + short a,i,u... → ḥ becomes corresponding vowel's homorganic fricative (o,e)
  • E.g.: शिवः + अहम् → शिवो'हम्
  • śivaḥ + aham → śivo'ham
  • ḥ + long ā,ī,ū... → ḥ becomes corresponding vowel
  • E.g.: तेजः + ईशः → तेज ईशः
  • tejaḥ + īśaḥ → teja īśaḥ

Sound changes before consonants

  • ḥ + voiceless consonants k,c,t,p... → ḥ becomes ś, ṣ, s
  • E.g.: योगः चित्त → योगश् चित्त / मनः + तापः → मनस्तापः
  • yogaḥ citta → yogaś citta
  • मनः + तापः → मनस्तापः
  • manaḥ + tāpaḥ → manastāpaḥ
  • ḥ + voiced consonants g,j,d,b... → ḥ becomes r
  • E.g.: प्राणः + गच्छति → प्राणर् गच्छति
  • prāṇaḥ + gacchati → prāṇar gacchati
  • ḥ + nasals ṅ,ñ,ṇ,n,m... → ḥ drops, nasal lengthens
  • E.g.: शान्तिः + न → शान्तिं न
  • śāntiḥ + na → śāntiṃ na

Retention at sentence end or pause

ḥ with no following word or needing pause, retains original sound

Examples:

ॐ शान्तिः शान्तिः शान्तिः

oṃ śāntiḥ śāntiḥ śāntiḥ
